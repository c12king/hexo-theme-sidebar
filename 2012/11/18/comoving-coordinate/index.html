Comoving Coordinate

Nov 18 2012

In Cosmology, the mostly used coordinate system is the comoving coordinate. In this coordinate, our earth is always at the origin. Any object does not move in the coordinate when it stays at rest. So stars far away is not leaving from us with respective to their positions in such coordinate. What changes along the time axis as the result of the expansion of the universe is nothing but the space-time metric. We call the distance between two points with respective to the comoving coordinate, the ''comoving distance'' $r$ between the two. We call the distance calculated from the space-time metric the ''proper distance'', $$ d s = a(t) d r, $$ which changes along the time and is the real physical distance between two points.

Light we recieved on the earth right now $t_0$ is emitted at different times at different comoving distances. And the redshift of the light is related to its emition time $t_e$ by $$ \frac{a(t_0)}{a(t_e)} = 1 + z. $$ This enable us to relate the redshift to the comoving time axis and the comoving distance. Instead of saying ''An even happens at time $t_e$'', we could say ''An even happens at redshit $z$''. When we specify the redshift of the event, we also specify the comoving distance between the place it happens and the earth.

An important quantity defined in the comoving coordinate is the comoving volume per redshift at redshift $z$, \begin{eqnarray} \label{eq:ComovingVolumePerRedshift} \frac{d}{d z} \left(\frac{4\pi r^{3} }{3} \right) &=& 4\pi r^{2} \frac{d r}{d z} = 4\pi r^{2} \frac{d a}{d z} \frac{d t}{d a} \frac{d r}{d t} \nonumber \\ &=& 4\pi r^{2} \frac{-1}{(1+z)^{2}} \cdot \frac{1}{\dot{a}} \cdot \frac{c}{a} \nonumber \\ &=& - 4\pi r^{2} c \left( \frac{\dot{a}}{a} \right)^{-1} = - 4\pi r^{2} c H^{-1} (t_e(z)) \end{eqnarray}

where $ H(t)$ is the hubble constant at time $t$ in Eqn. \ref{eq:ComovingVolumePerRedshift}.
